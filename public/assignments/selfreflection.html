Persuasive Essay Self Reflection:

1. What did you learn about writing a thesis statement throughout this process?
Throughout this process, I learned how thesis statements should be formatted. There were a lot of rules that govern how a thesis statement should look, such as excluding facts and information. Also, thesis statements are supposed to be strong, as they can be thought as a "blueprint" for the rest of the essay. This is why we don't use facts and don't state conventional wisdom. But it would be best to find an equilibrium where the thesis isn't too wordy, yet contains enough information for readers to understand what the essay is talking about. If the thesis statement is too vague, the information in the rest of the essay will not support the thesis that well.

2. What did you learn about writing an introductory paragraph throughout this process?
In the introductory paragraph, I learned that there should be a balance on the amount of information you should put, similar to the thesis statement. If there isn't enough information, the reader may not fully understand what you are trying to say. For example, if you are talking about why the Blue-Legged Tasmanian Hippo is amazing, the reader may be confused what such animal is. At the same time, if you provide too much information, the rest of your essay won't expand on your point, so it's basically useless. Also, because introductions should be short to answer the reader's questions after the hook, that information will become compacted, and the reader may miss some of the information, making it obsolete.

3. What did you learn about writing a topic sentence throughout this process?
Topic sentences are like thesis statements for body paragraphs; they are blueprints that guide the paragraph. Everything in a body paragraph should refer to the topic sentence, which means by reading the topic, they know exactly what information and evidence will be present. Also, a golden rule in essay writing is that you should not use weak words (maybe) and casual/conversational phrases (This will be about...). This holds true for topic sentences. And again, there should not be facts in the topic sentence: else the body paragraph will have to only talk about that fact.

4. What did you learn about body paragraphs throughout this process?
Body paragraphs are more than just words that express what the author believes. They also contain examples, evidence, and explanations. These support the information and make it more solid, which helps persuade readers. Supports work best when they are from reliable sources, which is why body paragraphs (and the rest of the essay) should not contain any pronouns. Not only do they appear weak as evidence, but they may not represent the entire audience (as in we, they, you).

5. What did you learn about writing a concluding statement throughout this process?
It is important to conclude your essay well. With a good conclusion, the essay feels whole and complete, and the reader knows that there are no more points to be made. The concluding statement should also bring the reader's attention back to the topic/thesis as a reminder to what the author is trying to convey and the purpose of the whole essay.

6. What did you learn about the call to action?
The call to action is where the essay turns to the reader. This call to action can not only reinforce the topic, but also give readers the ability to do something. This is very powerful, as when one is presented with the chance to act, they are more strongly persuaded to believe in why they should do so. In my essay, I had some difficulty writing the call to action as this is where I have to refer to the reader without using pronouns.

7. Reflect upon your essay. What are 3 things you feel like you did well / accomplished with this unit and 1 thing you feel like you struggled with / wish you changed for this unit? Use your peer review forms as guidance for this question.
Some things I feel like I did well are find sufficient evidence for the essay. In most of my peer reviews forms, I have not been scolded for my evidence, and most of them say that the information helps advance the conversation. Anothing thing I did well was choose the right topic. Yes, you could say that this topic is a classic, but I could find a lot of information and I rarely struggled to find something to put. Finally, I felt good on the balancing of my essay. There weren't too many or too little facts either. There was a lot of things I wanted to talk about, but I narrowed it down to a few to not bore readers.

Something that I struggled with was formatting my essay. For a major portion of the term I didn't even have my explanations written. There were also some more minor problems such as incorrect word usage and improper transitions. I struggled with figuring out where to put information and eventually I moved entire paragraphs. In the end, I think I managed to finish okay.
